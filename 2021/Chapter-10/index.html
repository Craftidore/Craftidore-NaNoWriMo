Chapter 10

Monday/Tuesday, November 7th/8th, UniYear 137 — Arc 1

Screeeeeech.

Dismounting from her bike, Evelyn unslung her purse, pulling out the bike chain. Fastening her bike to the rack, she spotted her neighbor coming down the stairs.

"You have a visitor," her neighbor said. "Whomever it was had a key and let themselves in.

"But I haven't given anyone a key," Evelyn thought out loud, thoughts of her bike taking second place. She rushed up the stairs, fumbled for her keys, and unlocked the door.

Kirk sat in the middle of the living room, head buried in a book from Evelyn's shelf. "You have good taste," he commented.

"Normally, you give me a letter telling me when and where to meet you," Evelyn said. "Today, I find you in my apartment—my locked apartment, which, according to my neighbor, you had a key for—with no warning. Why? Also, how?" Evelyn asked, dumbstruck and reeling, as her brain reengaged.

"You're the first person this year to give me a true challenge. Nothing—NOTHING!—is known about this man. He has no history, no point when he entered or left the city, no impact. If I hadn't seen him with my own eyes, I would have sworn he didn't exist."

"Does this mean I don't owe you anything?" Evelyn asked, both hopeful and fearful of the answer.

"Not even Mr. Mystery here could truly escape me," Kirk told her, his tone informative, not prideful. "He hasn't given his name out, to anyone, as far as I can tell. He seems to be shopping for a place to hole up. He's settled under a bridge, using some sort of tent." He gave her the bridge address.

"That still seems sparse," Evelyn commented.

"You asked for the impossible, and I delivered," Kirk stated flatly. "I do have a bit more," he told her, handing her a set of papers. "Little observations, places where he's been spotted, etc."

"I suppose that'll have to do," Evelyn said.

"Tell you what," Kirk continued. "You found a request near impossible even for me. It was the most fun I've had in ages. I'll give you a five percent discount for that; that's five percent off my rate for basic information, given that's all I could come up with."

Evelyn knew negotiating with Kirk would be a waste of both their times and if she refused to pay, he'd never sell her his services again, so she wrote him his check, handing it over regretfully.

"So nice to do business with you deary," he said as he left.

***

Evelyn glanced at the address. She'd written it in her notebook, just in case she lost Kirk's papers. She began to regret her decision, as her mind drifted from her interview to the mystery before her.

"Are you even listening to what I have to say?" her interviewee asked, noticing that she'd stopped taking notes.

"I'm sorry, it's been a long day, and my brain is on the fritz," she said.

He replied, "So, really, there's no point to this interview."

"No, not that. What you have to say is interesting and important. I'll try to stay awake harder," she said.

Sighing, he said "Fine," and continued his explanation of the economic trends caused by the changes in paper supplies.

She managed to mostly stay awake through the rest of the interview, but she rushed through formalities when they had concluded, eager to get out the door. Half-stumbling as she mounted her bike, she decided to take a nap before investigating the bridge. Hopefully, he wouldn't move before then.

***

Groggily raising her head from the pillow, Evelyn checked the clock. It read two hundred. I must have been more tired than I realized Evelyn reflected. She debated going back to sleep, then decided on a compromise of a midnight snack. Sneaking out to the kitchen, staring at the refrigerator for a period of time her mother would be annoyed by, she grabbed the supplies for a light sandwich. After piecing it together, upon slight reflection, she also pulled out the mustard, applying it liberally.

As she sat down on a chair that had, against all expectation, remained clear of unpacked but unorganized clutter, her eyes drifted to the papers Kirk had left her. They aren't going to read themselves she reasoned. I can't read them during the day; work's been too hectic, with one interview after another, unending. She reached out for the papers with slightly mustardy hands, not caring whether she dirtied them.

She skimmed them first, determining the lay of the land. The first four pages were filled with sightings; location, time, source, and any available contextual information. Skipping past it, she found Kirk's conclusions.

"I genuinely don't know how this man got into the city," Kirk's papers said. "No one has seen him before two days ago, and I've got a running list of everyone who entered this city in the past three years; he's not on it. In light of this revelation, and factoring in the actions he has made, I decided that when he arrived was the wrong question, and instead treat him as spontaneously bursting into existence yesterday.

"This isn't the first time I've had to do this. About three months ago, I was set on a similar case and found a man for whom no knowledge was to be had prior to a certain date. In that case, I was hired by the subject in question, as he was hoping to acquire a measure of how well he was able to cover his tracks."

Delightful Evelyn thought to herself. Kirk didn't usually include details about his prior business in his reports. On the one hand, what he had to say was fascinating, but on the other, Evelyn was impatient to know more about Mr. Vacuum.

"I think the subject, based on the reports I've received on his conversations, is unfamiliar with Gerian culture. He stumbles when asked what fief he is from, and doesn't understand a number of idioms, but hasn't claimed to be from another country. Surprisingly, he doesn't have an accent.

"The real curiosity comes from the subject's thirst for knowledge. He's conducted at least 5 study sessions inside various libraries in and near the city. I managed to sneak an agent in for two of his study sessions. He appeared to be researching recent technological advances, usually based on country. He seemed to be primarily focused on vacuum chambers, regularly cross-referencing books that talked about them.

"Finally, he was always wearing a bulky black armband. I have no clue why, but he seems unwilling to remove it, although he clearly finds it uncomfortable to wear."

By the second to last paragraph, she was starting to nod off. By the end of the final one, Evelyn had fallen asleep, the final bite of the sandwich still resting in her hand.

***

Evelyn strode up to the bridge. She'd endured another day of work, followed by two more interviews, and she wasn't going to put this off any longer, no matter how tired she was. The bridge loomed before her, its frame miraculously supporting the fleet of cars traveling across it. Below it, the river Kij rushed, rocky rapids unsurpassable. Surveying, she remembered that Kirk only mentioned Mr. Vacuum spent the night here, but that he spent his days doing research. Cursing her forgetfulness, she trudged back to her bike and rode back home. This time grabbing a lawn chair and a novel, she strapped the former onto her bike, zipping the latter into a pouch attached to the front of it.

This time, Evelyn was prepared. She located a nice shady spot not quite under the bridge, but still on the concrete foundation beneath it. Setting up her chair, she got to reading, completing five chapters.

***

Kient, unaware of his monikers as "the subject" or "Mr. Vacuum," finished with his research, had finally started developing a game plan. Time travel was limited to vacuum chambers, as the time machine could not displace the air from the past. If he time traveled anywhere except a vacuum he'd die from the sudden doubling in density. Kient and the time machine's inventor, Aarn Krons, had spent a ridiculous amount of time researching for a time in history when a large—human-sized—vacuum chamber was used, but at a time with few or no witnesses. The sixth of November in UniYear 137 had been the first one they'd come across.

His game plan, still taking shape in his mind, was to start building a nest egg of sorts, saving funds until he had enough to basically go into retirement. During this time, he'd have to let someone in on his secret. Specifically, he'd need someone from this time to guard over a vacuum chamber, creating a "home base" of sorts, a place and time where it was always safe to time travel to.

I really need to get a better place to hole up every night Kient thought as he approached his chosen bridge, night falling. He'd brought a compact sleeping bag that came with a built-in heater, which made the bridge he slept under far less unbearable. Nonetheless, he felt his living accommodations could use significant improvement.

Using his in-eye scanner, he mapped out the path to his current lodgings. Sitting, seemingly enjoying the view, a woman, evidently tall, even while sitting, scanned the perimeter. As he passed her, she spoke up. "Wait, sir."

Stopping, Kient turned to her. "Yes?"

"Look, I know this is a bit awkward," she began. He thought she looked familiar, but his brain stubbornly refused to place her. A flicker of motion caught Kient's eye and he grabbed the woman's hand, pulling her to the ground as the crack of a bullet echoed over the rushing water.

In-eye scanner working overtime, Kient found the source of danger. Standing on top of the bridge, handgun pointed down, a man Kient couldn't make out, even with his scanner, fired a second time.

The woman, who'd managed to locate the shooter as well, despite her lack of vision-enhancing technology, stood up, pulling Kient after her as she ducked beneath the bridge overhang, out of his line of sight. Kient set his backpack down, digging through it for his handgun, an antique he'd brought for its inconspicuousness, breaking free of the woman's surprisingly strong grip, and ran back out into the night. Studying and scanning the bridge, he realized that the mysterious shooter had disappeared.
